# Drift — the living world: how everything is born, lives, and dies

*A plain-language tour of the Drift ecosystem, reconstructed from the server simulation (`server/world-do.js` + `server/systems/` + `server/seed.js`). Every number here is a real tuning value from the code. Written for the world's maker — with an eye to the goal: a thriving, interconnected ecosystem.*

> **The one rhythm behind all of it:** the world advances one **tick** every 60 seconds — one tick is one "minute" of world-time — whether or not anyone is watching. Almost everything below happens *per tick*. A season lasts ~120 ticks (~2 hours of ticks); a full year of four seasons ~8 hours. The world is patient.

---

## Part 1 — the life of each thing

### Plants & seeds

**Born.** Seeds arrive in the world in two ways: through the initial seeding at launch (approximately 62% of the ~1,700 starter objects scattered across groves), or born from mature plants shedding them continuously throughout the world's breathing. A mature plant sheds a new seed roughly every 6 ticks (every 6 minutes of world time) when it's thriving and the local patch isn't crowded. The giant gardener also actively sows seeds in clearings, and when two people linger together in the same patch, the world blossoms—conjuring 2–3 fresh flowering plants born already mature, sometimes gifting a luminous anomaly too. Anomalies can also work wonders: drop one on a young plant and it instantly ripens to maturity. Seeds land randomly between 55 and 160 world units from their parent, scattered to prevent regrowth clumping. If a seed lands in water, it's gently nudged to the nearest bank to root on land.

**Lives.** A seed's life follows a continuous, four-stage maturity ladder, each stage shaped by a 0–1 value called `maturity` that grows each 60-second tick. The journey: maturity below 0.14 = dormant seed (invisible to warmth); 0.14–1.0 = sprout (visibly swelling); maturity = 1.0 = a rooted plant; then aged 0–1 = slow decline toward dissolution. Growth is glacially slow unattended—0.0016 maturity per tick (~10 hours to reach full maturity from a bare seed)—but warmth from nearby people accelerates it dramatically: +0.055 maturity per tick when heat is at full strength, taking just ~18 minutes with someone lingering nearby. Season modulates this heartbeat too: during Growing season plants gain 100% normal growth; during Turning they slow to 25%; Resting brings growth nearly to halt (0%); Rising brings it back to 60%. Heat itself lingers: an object retains 80% of its warmth each tick when nobody's near, so tended plants keep their momentum even between visitors. An anomaly nearby acts as a local accelerant: seeds gain an extra 0.02 maturity per tick within ~200 world units of a wonder. Once maturity reaches 1.0, a plant stops growing and begins aging at 0.0045 aged per tick (~many hours to fully age). While aging, a mature plant sheds seeds about every 6 ticks—but only if its patch isn't crowded. Density-dependent reproduction is strict: count same-family plants within 160 units; below 4 neighbors it sheds freely (probability 1.0); at 9+ neighbors shedding is fully suppressed (probability 0); between 4–9 it fades linearly, so the world spreads outward rather than thickening into impenetrable thickets. A plant stops shedding once it reaches aged = 0.6. Anomalies slow aging too: plants within 200 units of a wonder age at only 40% the normal rate, stretching their productive life. A tree at full maturity (and rooted after 80% maturity) is immovable—it becomes a landmark people can drag across to pan the world. Held objects (being carried) pause all growth, giving people a moment of control over the world's breath. The tick also refreshes the `last_touched` clock with any warmth above 0.02, so tended plants avoid isolation decay and survive longer.

**Dies.** When aged reaches 1.0, a plant dissolves. Before vanishing, it releases a final gift: 2 seeds scattered around it (same 55–160 unit radius as regular shedding, bank-settled if they land in water). This is the only "death release"—the world's way of ensuring a mature plant gets one last chance to seed even if crowding has suppressed its regular shedding. Once dissolution happens, the plant is gone from the world entirely, removed from storage. Stones don't age this way (they only erode from handling), but seeds can be forcibly evicted early: when the world is at the population ceiling (10,000 objects by default), the longest-untouched free objects are trimmed back under the cap so a packed world keeps breathing. Seeds and crystals are trimmable; anomalies, creatures, and fish are protected. A forgotten stone sitting untouched and unwarmed for ~a day (1,440 ticks) crumbles to grit and vanishes—isolation reclaiming what the world has abandoned. However, anomalies are immortal: they never age or dissolve, only dissolve if deliberately held for 10 seconds (a human choosing to release them). The checkpoint system means that between discrete life events (births, deaths, reclaims), growth and aging drift sub-threshold for ~30 minutes, batched to storage when the checkpoint fires—so the world's write rate scales with change, not population, keeping the 10,000-object dream feasible.

**Woven to —**

- **Warmth & Heat System:** Heat from nearby people accelerates growth dramatically (0.055 extra maturity/tick at full heat). Heat decays at 80% retention per tick when nobody's near. An object retains this heat as a vector for growth acceleration and measures presence within ~240 world units. The thermal field also slowly forms stones in sustained-warm spots, linking plant growth to environmental effects.
- **Seasons Clock:** Season phase (Growing/Turning/Resting/Rising, cycling every ~2 hours) modulates both growth and aging rates. Growing: 1.0× growth, 0.7× aging. Turning: 0.25× growth, 1.4× aging. Resting: 0% growth, 0.3× aging. Rising: 0.6× growth, 0.8× aging. The season advances 4/480 per tick, advancing the whole world's color grade and lifecycle cadence together.
- **Water Flow & Ponds:** Seeds mature enough to have rooted (≥ maturity 0.14) drift slowly along the water current. Seeds that land or drift into a pond are nudged to the nearest bank each tick so they never take root in water. Water flow is shaped by a deterministic noise field and deflected tangentially around stones, channeling objects in the same directions that water 'flows' visually.
- **Anomalies (Luminous Wonders):** An anomaly within ~200 units of a seed quietly speeds its growth (+0.02 maturity/tick) and slows its aging (40% normal rate), extending productive life. Drop an anomaly on a young plant, and it instantly ripens to full maturity = 1.0, aged = 0. Drop one that also bursts, and the mature plant shatters into 3–5 fresh saplings (age 0.06–0.12), scattering growth.
- **Communion & Shared Wonder:** When two+ people linger together within ~300 world units for at least 2 ticks, the world blossoms at their midpoint: 2–3 flowering plants spawn instantly at high maturity (0.82–1.0), and 30% of the time an anomaly is born—a rare, glowing wonder that emerges only from shared attention. This is the 'reward for tending together.'
- **Giant Gardener (NPC):** The giant tends the world by sowing seeds in clearings where there are no plants within ~220 units, and by thinning crowded patches to prevent the world from becoming an impenetrable thicket. It actively shapes where seeds can take root and reproduces, indirectly controlling density.
- **Isolation & Fade:** Seeds (like all plants) are exempt from isolation fade—they never crumble from being forgotten. Their lifecycle is purely time-based (warmth-accelerated growth/aging). Only stones fade when untouched and cold. This means a seed can sleep forever in a quiet corner and still bloom when someone eventually visits.
- **Ceiling Population Cap:** When the world reaches its population ceiling (~10,000 objects), the longest-untouched free seeds and crystals are trimmed to make room, but anomalies, creatures, and fish are always protected. Seeds are thus first to be reclaimed when the world is full and breathing room must be made.

> **Where the thread is thin →** The seeding→shedding→crowding loop is one-directional: mature plants shed but the giant is the only NPC that actively sows in empty clearings. Without the giant's intervention, a patch could saturate and then never unsaturate (once SHED_DENSITY_MAX is hit, no seeds escape). The giant's sowing is the thin link that lets reproduction reach isolated clearings. Conversely, there's no seed dormancy mechanism—a seed always grows if tended (warmth). A "pause growth, wait for better conditions" strategy doesn't exist, so seeds can't cluster intelligently. The link between density suppression and anomaly spawning is also thin: anomalies spawn rarely (3% per tick), only near mature plants (aged < 0.5), and only in generative seasons (Growing/Rising). A world saturated with crowded patches gets no anomaly blessing—the rare gift only appears in balanced, thriving zones. Finally, the isolation fade is stone-only; seeds are immortal once born. This means the ceiling is the only pressure that removes seeds, and the ceiling trims indiscriminately by age, not quality. A slow, valuable seed that happens to be old gets trimmed just as fast as a worthless one.

### Stones

**Born.** Stones are born in two ways. The primary way is spontaneous: in the thermal system, whenever a region stays warmly occupied long enough, the world crystallizes. A cell of the invisible heat field that holds temperature above 0.35 (on a 0–1 scale) for an uninterrupted 90 server ticks (~1.5 minutes of real time, each tick being 60 seconds) will slowly form a stone. But there's a crucial catch: the cell must be unattended — no person present warming it. The maker is long gone before the stone finishes (systems/heat.js, line 80–85). When the threshold is crossed, a new stone record is created at a randomized location within that heat cell, its seed drawn from the noise function that determines all its physical properties forever after. The second way is through breaking: when someone double-taps an existing stone, it shatters into 2–3 smaller pieces, each with a fresh seed but area-conserved via the radius (lines 1515–1529). These newborns scatter around the break point, each at least 8 world units in radius (the floor — MIN_STONE_R).

**Lives.** A stone's existence is shaped by two competing forces: growth-by-fusion and erosion-by-handling. When placed on top of another stone, the two fuse: their areas combine (via Pythagorean addition of radii) into a single larger stone that lives at the target's position. But there's a ceiling: hand-fused stones cap at radius 350 world units (STONE_CAP_R, tunable; lines 214–215). Exceed that and they simply bounce off each other — the dropper rebounds, unmerged. The fused stone's timestamp of last_touched is refreshed, marking it as tended. Meanwhile, every time a stone is handled — picked up and placed — it wears by one increment, tracked in a `handling` counter (line 1003). After 26 handlings (GRIT_HANDLING, tunable; line 207), the stone has eroded to dust. On its 27th placement, it crumbles into grit, vanishes from the world, and leaves a brief scattering visual effect for everyone to see (lines 1064–1067). While it waits to be handled, a stone's fate is tied to isolation and memory. A forgotten stone—one untouched and sitting in a place where the ambient heat field hasn't warmed it above 0.02 (WARM_EPS; line 66)—slowly accumulates an isolation debt. After a full day (1440 ticks, roughly 24 hours of accumulated world-time; STONE_FADE_MS line 67) with zero contact, it crumbles to grit as the world reclaims the forgotten (line 1361). Stones also participate in position wars: every tick, if another object (a plant, creature, or anomaly) overlaps a stone's footprint, it's gently pushed clear to just-touching, biased toward the downward direction (the front) so it settles against the near side rather than disappearing behind (lines 1625–1641). Finally, stones respect water: if one lands or drifts into a pond, it rolls out to rest on the nearest bank, pushed outward by its own radius so no part hangs over the water (lines 1645–1652). This rolling is broadcast with a roll flag so players see the stone ease out rather than snap.

**Dies.** Stones die by three mechanisms. First, grit-by-handling: after 26 handlings (each pick-up and placement increments the counter), the stone is worn to dust. On the 27th placement, it becomes grit and vanishes with a puff effect (lines 1064–1067). Second, forgotten-to-dust: a stone left untouched and unwarmed for a full day (1440 ticks; STONE_FADE_MS, tunable) crumbles to grit and is removed from the world (line 1361). This reclaim cycle is vital for the world's breathing room — an uncared-for stone doesn't linger forever. Third, there's the giant gardener's curation: unlike players who fuse up to a cap at radius 350, the gardener breaks down any stone larger than radius 62 (GIANT_BREAK_R, tunable; line 215), walking large boulders back toward a more balanced size. When either event removes a stone, it's deleted from the spatial grid and storage (lines 947–956), the broadcast ledger is cleared, and the dirt set is scrubbed. For grit and fade, the object simply vanishes. For the giant's breaking, the original stone is removed and 2–3 smaller children spawn in its place, scattered with fresh random seeds but area-conserved (lines 1515–1529).

**Woven to —**

- **Heat field:** Stones spontaneously form when a cell sustains warmth above 0.35 for 90 ticks while unattended; heat also refreshes the isolation clock (WARM_EPS), postponing fade
- **Water and pools:** Stones deflect flow tangentially (FLOW_STONE_R = 70 world units), channelling water around themselves; if they drift or land in a pond, they roll out to the bank
- **Giant gardener:** The gardener breaks stones larger than GIANT_BREAK_R (62 radius, tunable) back toward equilibrium; fuses small pebbles below STONE_EQ_R (40 radius) into cairns
- **Player interaction:** Players pick, carry, and place stones; each placement increments handling, eroding toward grit at 26 handlings; double-tap breaks a stone into smaller pieces
- **Other objects (plants, creatures, anomalies):** Stones win position conflicts: overlapping non-stone objects are pushed clear each tick; plants set down on stones settle beside them (Unit ⑥); stones never move
- **Spatial grid and ceiling:** Stones are indexed in the in-memory spatial grid for fast fuse/settle queries; forgotten stones contribute to the ceiling trim when the world is over capacity

> **Where the thread is thin →** The ecosystem of stones has a few underdeveloped seams. First, the flow-stone interaction is one-directional: stones deflect flow around themselves, but flow never breaks stones down (they only erode by handling and fade). A stone in a rushing current never fragmentizes. Second, stones don't directly interact with anomalies (the luminous wonders): they can't be powered up or transformed by them. An anomaly can work on plants and creatures but walks right past a rock. Third, there's no emergent geomorphology—stones don't settle into natural arrangements or influence each other's positions beyond the fuse rule; they could benefit from a slow settling physics where heavy stones sink slightly and cluster gravitationally. Finally, stones and the ceiling trim are somewhat orthogonal: very old forgotten stones are trimmed when the world is full, but there's no symbiosis where stone age or position guides trim priority—it's purely last_touched, uniform across all trimmable families."

### Creatures (crawlers & fliers)

**Born.** A creature is born in two ways: through spontaneous spawn or by mating. Every 60s tick, the world checks its creature population against a floor (50–120 total, split evenly across species to ensure a balanced mix) and a per-species floor (12 per kind) to prevent extinction. When the population is below the floor, a random creature type is spawned near a mature plant (seeded plants in a verdant area) or near the world's centre of gravity if the world is bare. Additionally, when two creatures of the same species drift within 64 world units of each other (the mate distance), they have a 5% chance per tick to breed. The offspring emerges 80–150 units away from its parents' midpoint (giving the new life breathing room), with a seed blended from both parents' genetics plus a small mutation, inheriting the parents' kind. Births stop at the 120-creature cap or when a species would exceed its per-species ceiling (60); so the population churns gently, never exploding.

**Lives.** Every creature is driven by a slow 5-tick cycle that rotates it through four states: feed (seek the nearest mature plant, or a favoured one via seeded affinity within ~260 world units), drink (seek the nearest pond's edge within ~720 units), rest (seek the nearest stone to settle beside within ~720 units), and roam (true free wandering, unless a person is lingering nearby within 760 units, in which case the creature ambles toward them out of curiosity, stopping 95 units away—curious but not crowding). Each tick, the creature's home (its stored x/y position, separate from the visible wander jitter) moves up to 46 units toward its current goal, or 130 units if it has been befriended by someone and they are within 1500 units (bonded creatures move 3× faster to keep up). Simultaneously, every creature is shoved away from any neighbour homes closer than 46 units—an anti-crowd force summed from all too-close neighbours that keeps the population a loose scatter, never a pile. A creature is very lightweight in the world's physics: it is pushed clear by stones, drifts along the water flow slightly, and can be picked up and carried like any object. When a bonded creature follows a person, it ignores all other drives and simply drifts toward them at its faster pace, settling about 70 units away. This bond lasts roughly 6 minutes (a real-time window long enough for a brief companion arc—befriending happens through sustained hovering attention in the UI). A creature has a visible "strength" derived from its kind (fliers are smaller, ~11–17 units; crawlers are larger, ~14–20 units) and randomness in its seed, so the bigger one always wins a clash. If someone places a crawler (land-dwelling creature) into a pond, it drowns instantly and becomes fish food: the creature vanishes and a fish rises in that pond if there is room. Ground bugs can also interact with anomalies (luminous, rare objects): dropping one on an anomaly, or placing an anomaly on one, triggers a power—a heart-shaped anomaly tames the creature (same effect as befriending—it follows the nearest person for ~2.5 minutes), and any other anomaly kind gives it a glowing buff that doubles its wander speed for ~3 minutes.

**Dies.** A creature dies through inter-species violence. When a crawler and a flier come within 58 units of each other, they have a 20% chance per tick to clash. The smaller or weaker of the pair is the loser; the loser has a 28% chance to die outright, but if death doesn't trigger, it instead flees—its home is teleported 130 units away in the opposite direction and it is re-indexed in the spatial grid so the rest of the world sees it bolt. Deaths are capped: a kind never drops below its floor (12 individuals), and the total population never goes below the baseline (50 creatures), so the world keeps breathing even if one species is being hunted. Most deaths are violent (inter-species clash), but a creature can also perish if dropped into water—crawlers specifically become fish food. The server-only death decision (no client-side reproduction, keeping the logic authoritative) is broadcast immediately as a removal message, and the creature record is flushed to storage.

**Woven to —**

- **Plants (seeds):** Creatures feed on mature plants as their primary drive goal, seeking them within ~720 units; plants shed seeds; creatures sometimes carry seeds in their jaws or on their bodies as they roam (visible flora-fauna dance)
- **Stones:** Creatures rest beside stones (the rest drive seeks them as goal targets); anti-crowd separation keeps them from piling on each other near boulders; stones deflect flow, which also affects creature drift
- **Ponds and water:** Drink drive pulls creatures to pond edges to sip; crawlers dropped in water become fish food and spawn a fish; the water flow gently nudges free creatures along
- **Anomalies (rare luminous objects):** Interact via drop: heart anomalies tame creatures (same as befriending, ~2.5 min bond), other anomalies give a glow buff (2× speed for ~3 min); anomalies are reusable (never consumed)
- **Giant (the gardener NPC):** No direct interaction, but the giant's tending and stone-moving shape the landscape that creatures roam through; the giant and creatures coexist but operate independently
- **Players / presence:** Creatures are curious—during roam phase, a creature nearby a lingering person ambles over to within 95 units out of curiosity; sustained attention befriends them (~4.4s dwell time UI-side); befriended creatures follow the nearest person for ~6 min; when befriended, person's location becomes the creature's only drive goal, overriding feed/drink/rest/roam entirely
- **Heat and warmth:** No direct creature effect, but nearby human presence warms objects and speeds plant growth; creatures feeding on warmed plants benefit indirectly
- **Seasons:** Creature drives and social behaviour are independent of season, but the world's growth/aging rates shift (affecting food availability) as seasons cycle through Growing/Turning/Resting/Rising (~2 hours per season unattended)

> **Where the thread is thin →** Creatures operate nearly independently of the gardener giant—they share the same world facade and spatial grid, but have no mechanic that binds their actions (e.g., the giant doesn't herd them, and they don't follow it or interfere with its tending). This is intentional simplicity, but a richer link could emerge: creatures could be attracted to recently-tended areas, or the giant could gently guide them toward uncrowded patches. The affinity-based target selection (seeded per-creature-per-tree to spread them) is a clever hack that declusters without centralised shepherding, but it's entirely invisible to the player—the prose of "creatures favour different trees" never reaches the UI. Anomalies interact richly with creatures (tame/glow), but only if dropped deliberately; there's no autonomy-side drive for creatures to seek anomalies out. Befriending is a 1:1 bond (creature follows nearest person), which works, but the world would bloom richer if a very-tended creature gained social bonds to other creatures (e.g., "form a small flock and move together"), or if the presence of a befriended creature drew others (a sort of "crowd magnetism" around companionship)—right now each creature is atomised. The tie between creature lifecycle and the heat/warmth system is mediated only by plants: creatures eat plants, plants grow faster when warm, so indirectly a person's presence feeds the creatures. A more direct link (e.g., "creatures near warmth gain a brief speed/hunger-reduction buff") would make human presence felt more immediately in the living swarm. Fish and creatures coexist but never interact except the "crawler-becomes-fish" conversion on pond drop; a more woven ecology (predator/prey, herding, schooling behaviour) would knit the two families together.

### Water, fish & crystals

**Born.** The world contains water in four stationary pools: a central pool of radius 350 at the origin (where objects naturally accumulate and flow gathers), plus three smaller ponds scattered at fixed positions across the world (world-do.js:148–153). The central pool is where crystals form. When the tick runs (every 60 seconds), crystalline formations are spawned at the pool's edge via a chance-based pass: each tick has a 5% chance to spawn a new crystal if the world holds fewer than 10. A crystal materializes as a deterministic random position at radius 0.82–1.12 times the pool's radius (82–112% of the edge), along the elliptical rim defined by POND_ASPECT (world-do.js:1781–1788). Fish hatch similarly—each pond is kept stocked to a floor of 3 fish and can hold up to 6. Every tick, any pond below floor triggers spawns until it reaches 3; between 3 and 6, an 18% random chance per tick spawns an additional fish (world-do.js:1411–1418). A fish's home sits strictly inside its pond, within 50% of the pond's radius from centre, so its bounded wander (computed clientside, but anchored by this home seed and wanderT0 timestamp) never breaks the water's edge. Fish are born alive—they skip isolation-fade, ceiling trim, and water drift (world-do.js:165–168).

**Lives.** Every tick, eligible objects—mature plants and crystals—creep along an invisible current. The water's flow is deterministic at every world point, derived from a shared noise field (the same one painted on the client's water sheen). This base flow direction is then bent around nearby stones within 70 world units; the deflection is tangential (curving the flow alongside the stone) plus a small radial-away term to keep the current from lodging against the rock (world-do.js:1663–1685). The flow also bends toward cooler areas, following the inverse heat gradient at a strength of 0.6, so warmth caches create gentle eddies (world-do.js:1678–1681). The flow speed is 1.6 world units per 60-second tick, but modulated by season: Growing (0.4×), Turning (0.85×, dispersing), Resting (0.05×, nearly frozen), Rising (1.0× full strength) (world-do.js:232–239). Objects drift only when free and unheld; a pre-sprout seed must root before it creeps—only once maturity ≥ 0.14 does a plant become eligible (world-do.js:1690–1699). Drift is incremental: position updates are broadcast only once an object has crept 6 world units from its last broadcast point, so pools don't spam state; sub-threshold creep is marked dirty and persisted at the checkpoint (~30 minutes) rather than per-tick (world-do.js:1326–1351). Crystals decay very slowly as they exist. Each tick, crystal decay advances by 1/300, so a crystal takes ~300 ticks (~5 hours) to fully dissolve, at which point it vanishes with a brief flash (world-do.js:1271–1275; CRYSTAL_DECAY = 1/300, line 171). Fish do not drift or decay; they are alive and tended. Their position is not stored—only their home and the wander anchor time (wanderT0) are persisted (world-do.js:88–93). They swim in a bounded, deterministic pattern computed from their home position and a shared clock; clients derive the live position each frame, keeping the always-ticking world free from position updates (world-do.js:162–163).

**Dies.** Crystals dissolve in place. When decay reaches 1.0, they are removed from the world in a moment of dissolution (world-do.js:1271–1275). Fish are spared the ceiling population trim and isolation fade; they live as long as their pond remains below the cap. When a bug (crawler creature) is dropped into a pond, it becomes fish food—it vanishes with a splash animation, and if the pond has room (below 6 fish), a new fish immediately rises (world-do.js:1004–1017; FISH_MAX_PER_POND = 6). Stones that drift into a pool roll back to the nearest bank, pushed out by the world's physics—a rock never stays in water (world-do.js:1644–1652, 1436–1458). Seeds that land in water are nudged to the bank each tick if they remain (world-do.js:1434–1458). Pools are also the natural sink: the world's flow field gently directs objects toward the central pool's low point, where the current becomes deterministic yet benign—drift-eligible objects creep along the edges and eventually settle, becoming part of the slow, quiet water-life. Crystals eventually dissolve completely as their 5-hour clock expires; fish are never evicted naturally, only consumed by the food chain or the population ceiling (world-do.js:1368–1380).

**Woven to —**

- **Plants (seeds):** Drift-eligible plants (maturity ≥ 0.14) creep along the water flow when near the central pool, moving up to 1.6 world units/tick with seasonal modulation. Plants can be nudged out of ponds to the bank if they land in water.
- **Stones (rocks):** Stones act as walls to the flow (deflectsFlow: true). The water bends tangentially around them at 70-unit radius, creating persistent channel patterns. Stones that enter a pool automatically roll to the bank, never staying in water.
- **Creatures (bugs):** Crawler creatures dropped into a pond become food (the creature vanishes with a splash), triggering a fish spawn if the pond has room (below 6).
- **Heat field:** The water flow bends toward cooler areas at strength 0.6. Sustained warmth creates eddy zones that redirect the current, sculpting how objects meander.
- **Seasons:** Water flow speed scales by season: Resting (0.05×, nearly still), Growing (0.4×, gentle), Turning (0.85×, dispersing), Rising (1.0×, full strength). The season also modulates crystal formation and decay indirectly through drift patterns.
- **World ceiling & population:** Fish and crystals are spared the ceiling trim (population ceiling never evicts them). Fish occupy 'tended' slots (alive, never faded). Crystals are trimmable but decay naturally over ~5 hours regardless of capacity.
- **World broadcast & write-economy:** Crystal decay and plant drift are sub-threshold changes: they ride the ~30-minute checkpoint rather than writing per-tick. Broadcasts only fire once an object drifts 6+ units, keeping network chatter low even in a pool-full of slow drifters.

> **Where the thread is thin →** The system is thin in two directions. First, **fish have no emergent behaviour beyond spawning and wander**—they are purely aesthetic and food-chain endpoints. They don't age, grow, interact with each other, respond to plants or creatures, or influence the world except as a reward for tending (dropping bugs). They could be enriched with simple hunger/satiation, schooling, or plant-grazing to make them active agents rather than visual dressing. Second, **crystals are decorative with no purpose beyond existence**—they don't interact with objects, can't be picked up or used for anything, and don't affect growth or flow. Crystals could gain agency: e.g., nearby objects could attract them for collection, they could emit gentle heat, or breaking them could release seeds. The water flow itself is also one-directional: it affects eligible objects but never interacts back—objects don't create eddies, stones don't wear, and flow strength doesn't respond to population density or object movement. Enabling reciprocal feedback (e.g., dense drifts slow the flow, or fast drift erodes banks) would deepen the water's role as an active ecosystem rather than a scenic utility.

### The gardeners (the two giants)

**Born.** Each Gardener awakens on world load, positioned near the centre-of-gravity (the world's heart) with a slight offset in opposite directions — one biased to the upper-right (+1000, +560), the other to the lower-left (−1000, −560) — so they naturally favour opposite sides of the world. They carry no memory or persistent state (positions reset in memory on each load), only a simple working mind: a heading vector, a current goal, a stuck counter, and a set of recently unreachable objects they've abandoned. The two are kept alive by the tick itself; they have no death or removal — as long as ticks run, the Gardeners walk.

**Lives.** Each tick (every 60 seconds), a Gardener follows a rhythmic cycle. It can resolve up to 5 waypoints per tick, never standing idle — it either walks or works, never both. If it has no goal or its current goal becomes invalid (the object was held, removed, or no longer matches the job's criteria), it PICKS a new goal using a need÷distance-weighted scoring system. Pressing jobs (ripen, thin, fill holes, merge stones, break boulders) are scored first by (need × random jitter) ÷ (1 + distance/700): a thicket of 12+ plants within 160 world units (overcrowded, need=3.0) will pull a Gardener across the field past a lone sapling waiting to ripen. If nothing pressing is nearby, it cycles through idle jobs (sow, drink, watch) in rotation. If truly nothing nearby, it explores the whole world, roaming 60% toward a random point and 40% toward its home territory to keep the pair spread out. Movement is a brisk 800 world units per tick along its heading, and the client glides it smoothly between ticks so it always looks like it's going somewhere. When blocked by a pond, the Gardener circumnavigates along its rim rather than ramming the bank — it steers tangent to the water, staying dry. If a goal sits unreachably far (a boulder on an island), the Gardener will pursue it for 4 ticks without getting closer, then give up and blacklist that object for 30 ticks so it doesn't waste cycles re-picking the same impossibility. The two Gardeners coordinate: the companion never targets a job its sibling has already claimed, and if the companion is clearly closer (within 80% of the first's distance), the first yields and lets the closer one take it — no pile-ups. When the two come within 500 world units of each other, the one farther from its home territory yields space and peels off back to its side, so they naturally spread across the world. Once a Gardener reaches a goal (within 64 world units), it performs the job (if one exists) and checks if it can do another in the same tick — up to 2 tending jobs per tick, so a clustered world sees faster throughput. The jobs are: (1) RIPEN — touch a seed with maturity 0.02 to 0.99, jump it to 1.0 (full maturity) and reset its age to 0; (2) THIN — remove an overcrowded plant (≥12 same-family neighbours within 160 units); (3) FILL HOLE — remove a ground mark (a player's double-click drawing); (4) TEND STONE — nudge a small stone (radius &lt;40) onto a nearby pebble partner within 78 units, merging them into one larger stone (cairn-building); (5) BREAK STONE — smash a boulder (radius &gt;62) into 3–5 middling pieces; (6) SOW — seed a sparse spot (no plant within 220 units) with a young sprout at maturity 0.08–0.13. Non-tending goals (drink, watch, stroll) are pure pauses: the Gardener stands at the water's edge or beside a creature for a beat, then walks on. The Gardener reads live-tunable knobs — reach (64), sight (1300), break threshold (62), and the stone equilibrium radius (40) — so an operator can tune its behaviour on the fly without restarting the world. Growth phases (seasons) affect goal-picking indirectly only via the world's density and age, not the Gardener's own rate.

**Dies.** Gardeners never die in the conventional sense. They persist for as long as the world ticks. However, they can be TOGGLED OFF via the admin panel (/admin/giant?off=1), which disables both of them simultaneously — a testing tool to isolate write-economy behaviour or to pause their tending for observation. Their goal can be manually cleared (/admin/giant?x=&y=) to reset their position and make them pick a fresh goal immediately, simulating a disturbance. On a deploy or Durable Object eviction, the Gardeners' in-memory state (positions, goal, stuck counter) is lost, and they are re-initialized from the saved world centre-of-gravity on the next load — a soft reset with no persistence cost.

**Woven to —**

- **Seed plants (Family: seed):** Ripen young plants toward maturity when they reach 0.02–0.99 maturity; thin overcrowded family patches (≥12 neighbours within 160 units) to spread life outward and prevent thickets; sow sparse patches (no plant within 220 units) with young sprouts to breed life back where it's scarce. This is the balance mechanism — spreading where sparse, culling where dense.
- **Stones (Family: stone):** Merge small pebbles (radius &lt;40) into larger ones by nudging them together within 78 units (cairn-building, need=0.6, low urgency); break runaway boulders (radius &gt;62) into 3–5 pieces back toward equilibrium (need scales steeply with excess size, up to 4.5+). This is the equilibrium mechanism — keeping stone size in the middle zone.
- **Ground marks (Family: mark):** Remove marks (player double-click drawings) to keep the ephemeral surface from filling up. No need weight, low priority — tended only when no pressing jobs are nearby.
- **Ponds (POOLS):** Circumnavigate around pond bodies to avoid wading; identify pond rims as drink spots (a beat of rest by the water's edge, a reflective pause). Never enter the water — always steer around by tangent and backstop to the bank.
- **Creatures (Family: creature):** Occasionally stand and watch a nearby creature for a beat (the watch job, rotated in idle cycles). This is pure observation — no action, just the Gardener's curiosity. The watch goal is found within sight range (1300 units).
- **Seasons (season clock, SEASON_PER_TICK advances per tick):** The Gardener's goal-picking indirectly depends on the world's density and age (which are season-affected), but the Gardener's own logic and timings are season-agnostic. Stuck detection and avoid-blacklist expiry are keyed to tick count (season ÷ SEASON_PER_TICK).
- **Heat field (server/systems/heat.js, HeatField):** The Gardener does not read the heat field directly. Heat influences plant growth, which indirectly affects what jobs are available (a warmer plant ripens faster and may reach maturity sooner). The Gardener simply responds to what exists now.
- **World centre-of-gravity (cog, EMA of presence):** Each Gardener's home territory is offset from the cog (±1000, ±560), used to drive the pair apart. The cog is the point they begin near on load and the mid-point they roam around when exploring. It moves slowly with player presences (EMA weight 0.2), so the Gardeners naturally follow clusters without explicit attention.
- **Player presence (grid-based, tracked per tick):** The Gardener's behaviour is stateless relative to presence — it does not seek out players or avoid them. It simply picks goals from the living world and tends them, indifferent to watchers. A player is the underlying cause of many jobs (tending heat → ripening → aging → shedding), but the Gardener does not track or prioritize based on presence.
- **World object ceiling (maxObjects, default 10,000):** The Gardener's sow job checks the ceiling before spawning a seed — if the world is within 50 objects of the cap, it will not sow, leaving room to breathe. This is a self-regulating brake.
- **Companion Gardener (the sibling):** The two coordinate via claimed goals (never both pick the same id), distance-based yielding (closer one takes the job), personal-space separation (if too close, the one farther from home peels off), and opposing home-biases (naturally spread opposite sides). They are independent agents with shared logic but no explicit communication.

> **Where the thread is thin →** The Gardeners' interaction with the heat field is THIN: they do not read it, do not use it to prioritize work, and do not influence it directly. Heat is a hidden layer that accelerates plant growth and formation of stones; the Gardeners simply respond to the visible world (object densities, maturity values, stone sizes) that result. A richer ecology could have them respond to heat gradients (e.g., preferentially tend warm areas where growth is faster) or use the heat field to predict where new stones will form and pre-position themselves. The watch job is similarly isolated — standing beside a creature is pure aesthetic (the Gardener's curiosity), not a mechanism (it doesn't check if the creature is hungry or endangered, doesn't assist it or drive it away). Connection could emerge: a Gardener could prioritize tending areas where creatures cluster, or offer presence-based warmth bonuses, turning the Gardener from a passive balancer into an active partner in the ecosystem. The Gardeners also do not seed based on creature food availability — they sow where plants are sparse, not where creatures are hungry. A two-way link could tune the sow radius or frequency based on creature density in the area.

### Anomalies (rare wonders)

**Born.** An anomaly comes into being in two ways. The world rarely births one spontaneously during its "generative" seasons (Growing and Rising). Each tick, when the count of anomalies is below the limit (at most 4 exist at once), there's a 3% chance a mature plant (one that has finished sprouting and isn't yet aged) will spawn an anomaly nearby—roughly 30 to 90 world units away. The anomaly arrives fully-formed with a kind drawn randomly from five types: rotor, point, prism, breath, or heart. Alternatively, two people lingering together in the same patch can trigger a "communion bloom" (when they sustain togetherness for 2 ticks, then rest 4 ticks afterward). That bloom conjures 2-3 flowering plants and—a rare gift with 30% chance—births a luminous anomaly right there among them. Once born, an anomaly arrives as a free, unheld object carrying a seed (for form generation, never visually stored) and its kind. It persists until someone deliberately holds it for a full 10 seconds and dissolves it—a purposeful, witnessed act.

**Lives.** An anomaly does not grow, age, decay, or fade. It is "tended"—alive and immortal, immune to the isolation crumble that befalls forgotten stones. It sits where placed, indifferent to time and warmth. But it is anything but inert. Within a 200-world-unit radius, an anomaly silently quickens the world around it: seeds grow 0.02 maturity per tick faster (the ANOMALY_GROW_BOOST, speeding a plant's unfurling by roughly 8 hours of unattended growth), and aging slows to 40% its normal rate (ANOMALY_AGE_SLOW), letting mature plants linger longer before dissolving. This creates a zone of gentle beneficence—plants don't rush, but they take their time to flourish and age with grace. When you pick up an anomaly and carry it, a timer begins: 10 seconds of holding it will cause it to fade from your hands over 3 more seconds (the ANOM_DISSOLVE_MS and ANOM_FADE_MS). But before that endpoint, you can place it down and trigger its powers. An anomaly has four distinct "powers" that activate by kind, non-uniform in effect. If you drop an anomaly within 76 world units of a plant: a "point" or "breath" anomaly will RIPEN it (instantly mature an unripe seed to full maturity, or restore a partially-aged plant to youth). A "prism" or "rotor" anomaly will BURST a grown tree (maturity ≥ 0.14), shattering it into a scatter of 3-5 fresh saplings flung outward and automatically banked if they land near water—a sudden multiplication of life. If you drop an anomaly near a creature: a "heart" anomaly TAMES it, binding the creature to follow the nearest person within 1500 world units for 2.5 minutes. Any other kind makes the creature GLOW: it bathes in a rainbow hue and moves twice as fast for 3 minutes, a visible blessing. A hybrid anomaly (one fused from multiple kinds) wields all powers at once—a heart tames while also glowing, a prism-rotor combination both ripens and bursts in sequence. The anomaly itself is never consumed; it persists as a reusable wonder, ready to work its magic again elsewhere. Anomalies can FUSE: if you drop one atop another free anomaly within 76 world units, their kinds merge into a single hybrid object—its form blends visually, its powers compound, and the dropped anomaly vanishes. A fused anomaly can be broken apart by double-clicking it (like a stone) to split back into separate single-kind anomalies, each with a fresh seed, scattered around the break point.

**Dies.** Deliberate dissolution is the only death an anomaly knows. Hold it uninterrupted for 10 seconds (measured in wall-clock milliseconds—ANOM_DISSOLVE_MS = 10,000 ms). Over the next 3 seconds, it fades from your hands in a visual fade-out (ANOM_FADE_MS = 3,000 ms). Once those 3 seconds pass, it is gone—removed from the object map, no longer broadcasting to anyone. No seeds are released, no final gift; it simply ceases. This is the only way an anomaly can permanently vanish: by the holder's deliberate, sustained choice. It cannot be trimmed by the world's ceiling logic (anomalies are "tended," ceiling-protected). It cannot fade from neglect. It cannot age and dissolve. If a connection holding an anomaly closes, the hold is reclaimed after 45 seconds of silence, but the anomaly itself remains—newly free, ready for someone else to find and carry, or place and work its power again.

**Woven to —**

- **seed (plant family):** An anomaly within 200 world units silently accelerates growth (+0.02 maturity/tick) and slows aging (×0.4 rate), creating a generative halo. Drop an anomaly on a seed to ripen (mature it to full growth) or burst (shatter a mature plant into fresh saplings). Seeds reciprocate by having a 3% tick-chance (when mature, in generative seasons, under population cap) to spontaneously birth an anomaly nearby—a plant's rarest gift to the world.
- **creature (crawler/flier):** Drop an anomaly on a creature to tame it (heart kind makes it follow the nearest person for 2.5 min) or glow it (any other kind: rainbow hue + 2× speed for 3 min, a visible buff). Creatures cannot spawn anomalies, but they can be blessed by them. A creature's presence cannot sense or summon anomalies—only drops can trigger this power.
- **anomaly (itself):** Two free anomalies within 76 world units fuse when one is dropped onto the other—their kinds union (a hybrid), their form visually blends, and powers combine. A fused anomaly can be broken apart by double-click into separate single-kind anomalies, each with a fresh seed and scattered around the burst point. This is anomaly reproduction: merging to concentrate power, or splitting to spread it.
- **communion (shared human presence):** When two people linger together within 300 world units for 2 sustained ticks, the world blooms: 2-3 flowering plants spawn in the patch, and with 30% chance, a luminous anomaly joins them—the rarest reward for shared stillness, a gift for tending together.
- **stone (rock):** No direct interaction. Anomalies do not drift on water, do not fuse or break against stones, and stones do not affect anomalies. Anomalies occupy the world independently of rock geometry—they can be placed anywhere, even overlapping a stone's footprint.
- **hold / carry:** Only the current holder (the connection that picked up the anomaly, identified by token) can begin its dissolution timer. Carrying it for 10 uninterrupted seconds triggers fade-out; placing it resets the timer. If the holder's connection vanishes, the hold is reclaimed after 45 seconds, but the anomaly remains free (never auto-dissolves).
- **world ceiling (population cap):** Anomalies are ceiling-exempt ('tended'). They never trigger the world's trimming logic, so they do not count toward eviction when the population peaks. Up to 4 can coexist, protected from scarcity pressures.

> **Where the thread is thin →** The anomaly's relationship to discovery and rarity is thin. Anomalies spawn only when conditions align (generative season + mature plant + population cap space + 3% random chance), making spontaneous birth deeply rare and unpredictable. But once born, there's no mechanic to make them harder to find—no hiding, no distance decay in visibility, no mechanic that rewards searching long or looking far. A player who lingers in the world for an hour might see none, or stumble upon one within minutes. The thin seam here is that anomalies could be "called" (some attractor mechanic that nudges them toward human presence), so searching and patience felt rewarded, or conversely, they could "wander" slowly, making their encounter a matter of wandering skillfully rather than luck alone. Communion is the sole path to *guaranteed* anomaly manifestation, and it's elegantly constrained (shared presence, sustained ticks, cooldown), but the other 3% spawn is pure rarity—a welcome caprice, but one that invites no active play. The 10-second hold-to-dissolve mechanic is also one-directional: once you've picked it up, you're committed to either place it (reset timer) or wait it out. There's no "reluctant release" threshold or cost—dissolution is all-or-nothing after a passive wait. A mechanic where holding longer makes it harder to place (resisting your grip, pulling outward), or where you could choose *when* to start the dissolution timer (an active "let go" action), might deepen the feel of this rare object as something with its own agency.

### The world itself — seasons, heat & the population economy

**Born.** The world is seeded fresh with roughly 1,700 objects (seeds outnumber stones 62% to 38%), distributed across 22 groves scattered in a wide rectangle. The generator uses a relaxation algorithm to space them by their footprints — so a mature tree claims real room while tiny seeds pack closer — preventing the dense clumping that would stifle a young world. They're scattered, not centred; the heart grove stays tight so arrivals land near life, while the others breathe. This initial population grows over time toward a 10,000-object ceiling (tunable by environment; the PRD target). Every object arrives with a seed (a 32-bit deterministic form fingerprint), position, and lifecycle state — some seeds dormant, some stirring, some already mid-growth, a few already mature with a touch of age — so the world feels already in progress, not a barren void.

**Lives.** Every 60 seconds, the world ticks. This single, orderly heartbeat runs whether people are watching or not.

The season clock turns as you watch: a monotonic float that cycles through four seasons (Growing/Turning/Resting/Rising) over roughly 8 hours of ticks (world-do.js lines 105-108). Each season modulates how fast life grows and how fast it ages — Growing lets plants rush forward at full throttle while aging lingers; Resting freezes growth nearly still but ages things faster, as if time runs different in cold. The shift isn't abrupt; the seasons crossfade smoothly over the last third of each, so the colour grade and the world's rhythm shift gradually (line 276).

Thermal presence spreads invisibly. A person standing in the world adds heat to their location's coarse grid cell (200 world units wide). Heat at a location (0 to 1 scale) drives two things: it accelerates a seed's maturity gain per tick, and it bends the water flow subtly toward cooler areas. Heat decays each tick (retained at 0.80 or so, depending on season), so it lingers a short while after someone leaves but fades if no one returns (lines 43, 66-67, 238-239). A held object warms up near a person; a free plant nearby gains a bonus per tick from that heat — the warmth reward for lingering (line 40). Heat also forms stones slowly: when a cell stays warm AND unattended (the maker has left), it gradually progresses toward crystallizing a new stone (lines 74-85 in heat.js). This takes roughly 90 ticks (an hour and a half) of sustained warmth, so by the time the stone fully forms, the person who warmed the spot is long gone — the world remembers warmth as a gift.

Plants grow in two arcs. First, maturity rises from 0 to 1 (seed → sprout → young plant → full mature tree), accelerated by warmth and gentle anomalies nearby. An unattended plant grows one part per 1,700 ticks (~28 minutes) by base growth alone; with someone lingering nearby adding heat, that same plant matures in roughly 18 minutes. Once mature (maturity ≥ 1), the plant ages over 200+ ticks (~3+ hours at a moderate rate) from age 0 to 1, then dissolves — releasing a final pair of seeds as it goes (lines 38-48, 1290-1307). While mature and aging less than 0.6, the plant sheds seeds roughly every 6 ticks (line 46) — but density-dependent throttling suppresses shedding in crowded patches: below 4 neighbours of the same family it sheds freely, at 9+ it stops completely, and the range between is a smooth linear fade (lines 53-55). This spreads the world outward into new glades rather than thickening into impenetrable thickets. An anomaly nearby slows aging to a crawl (line 126) and adds a growth boost, so plants near a luminous wonder persist longer and shoot up faster.

The water field is always flowing. At every point on the map, an invisible current (determined by a shared noise field) drifts free objects very slowly — a few world units per tick — along a network of currents. Stones bend the flow tangentially around themselves, so water pools and eddies behind boulders (lines 233-238). The heat gradient also bends flow toward cooler areas, so drifters tend to migrate from warm patches toward cool ones. This is slow and gentle; a plant might take many ticks to drift a few dozen units, visibly creeping across the world (line 238). Objects in a pond (sitting in the water) don't drift; they're relocated to the bank each tick so nothing drowns (line 1436-1457).

Population is a hard ceiling with soft breathing. The world never exceeds 10,000 objects (or whatever the cap is set to). When the world reaches saturation, the longest-untouched objects — those that haven't been handled or warmed in the longest time — are gently evicted to make room for new life (line 1368-1379). This isn't catastrophic; it's like forgetting. A stone that sits untouched and unwarmed for a full day (1,440 ticks, or 24 hours of tick time) crumbles to grit and vanishes (line 67).

Marks (ground stains from double-clicks) are ephemeral. A mark persists for roughly 10 minutes, then fades and is removed (line 270). Crystals at the pool's edge form rarely and dissolve slowly over 5 hours, a brief shimmer of geometry (lines 170-171).

The creatures and fish (crawlers, fliers, swimmers) maintain their own sub-economy: the world keeps at least 50 creatures around (so you always spot some action) and caps them at 120 total, with roughly 3-6 fish per pond. They're born near plants, breed gently (5% chance per tick per eligible same-species pair within 64 units), clash with rivals (different species within 58 units, 20% clash chance, 28% chance someone dies), and follow their drives: feed on plants, drink from ponds, rest on stones, roam and hover around people out of curiosity (lines 24-46 in creatures.js).

The giant — the gardener — walks the world every tick, covering 800 world units per step. It tends the balance: ripening young plants (pushing them to maturity), thinning over-crowded patches (removing a plant from a cluster of 12+), filling marks (erasing people's scribbles), merging scattered pebbles into small cairns, breaking runaway boulders back toward the middle, and sowing seeds in empty patches (lines 30-48 in giant.js). Its decisions are need-driven: an egregious imbalance (a huge boulder, a thicket) pulls it from across the field; gentle work (a lone sapling) waits its turn. There are two gardeners, biased to opposite sides so they spread their care and avoid colliding.

Write economy is decoupled from broadcast. Every tick, the world broadcasts every lifecycle crossing (a seed sprouting, a plant ripening, aging, dying) to all watching clients so they see smooth growth. But those broadcasts don't each touch storage; instead, they're marked "dirty" and written together by a periodic checkpoint (every 30 real minutes) that flushes only the objects whose in-memory state diverged from disk (lines 70-98). Spawns and deaths still write immediately (discrete operations), so nothing born or gone is lost. But gentle growth — a seed's maturity creeping up by 0.016 per tick — rides the checkpoint. If the world crashes between checkpoints, that growth (at most 30 minutes' worth of slow change, perhaps 0.05 maturity) reverts to the last checkpoint. This is acceptable for a world this slow; it lets the always-ticking simulation stay off the rows-written quota and scale write cost with change, not population. The write ledger stays single-source (invariant #1): changes flow only through a TickContext that separates broadcast changes (deferred), reclaimed holds (immediate), spawns (immediate), and deaths (immediate), so nothing can slip through unmeasured (lines 307-330, 1463-1470).

**Dies.** Plants dissolve when aged to 1, releasing their final seeds. Stones crumble to grit after 26 handlings of wear, or after sitting untouched and unwarmed for a full day. Crystals fade over 5 hours, a slow dissolve. Marks heal and vanish after 10 minutes. When the world hits its ceiling (10,000 objects), the longest-untouched free objects (excluding anomalies, creatures, fish, held objects, and marks — these are "alive" and protected) are evicted oldest-first until there's breathing room again. Creatures and fish have their own turnover: they mate, clash, route or die, and are maintained within species-specific floors and caps so no kind goes extinct. Anomalies never fade (they're wonders, rare and persistent until someone dissolves one deliberately). Ground marks are the ephemeral layer — old scribbles fade away.

The isolation fade clock (last_touched, a real timestamp) drives everything that wears with disuse: a stone touched by a person gets a fresh clock; a stone warmed by nearby heat gets its clock refreshed (in memory at tick time, persisted at the checkpoint). This isn't a per-tick counter — it's a wall-clock timestamp — so the fade clock survives server restart, and the world's forgetting rate is stable regardless of how often it ticks. A forgotten stone sits for 1,440 ticks (a full day of tick time, roughly 24 real-world hours at 60s/tick) before it crumbles. The world is indifferent to whether you're watching: the seasons turn, the plants grow, the gardener tends, and forgotten things fade, whether anyone is online or the server sits quiet and ticking in the void.

**Woven to —**

- **Seasons (Growing/Turning/Resting/Rising):** Modulates growth rate (GROWTH_MULT per season) and aging rate (AGE_MULT per season), so the world's lifecycle rhythm changes as the season clock turns; also governs water flow magnitude (FLOW_SEASON) so water moves faster in Rising, slower in Resting. The season clock advances 4÷480 per tick, completing a full cycle roughly every 8 hours of ticks.
- **Thermal field (coarse grid of per-cell heat):** Heat at a location accelerates nearby seed maturity (ANOMALY_GROW_BOOST: +0.02 maturity per tick), bends water flow toward cooler areas (FLOW_HEAT coefficient), and sustains stone formation (cells ≥0.35 heat that stay unattended progress toward crystallizing a stone over 90 ticks). Heat decays seasonally so it lingers in Growing but bleeds away in Resting.
- **Presence (people in the world):** Each nearby presence (within HEAT_RADIUS = 240 world units) adds warmth to objects, accelerating their growth. Presences also refresh the last_touched clock when they're warm enough (heat > WARM_EPS), so a lingering person keeps nearby stones from fading. Presences warm the thermal field's grid cells, driving stone formation. The world's centre-of-gravity (cog) is an EMA of all pickup/place positions, used to seed creature births and to position the gardeners.
- **Objects (seeds, plants, stones, anomalies, creatures, crystals, fish, marks):** Every object is positioned on the spatial grid, drifts along water currents if free and nearby a pool, grows/ages if it's a seed, fades if forgotten and a stone, capped by the ceiling if trimmable, and tended by the giant if it's a pressing job. All lifecycle changes feed the broadcast cadence and dirty-set persistence system.
- **Creatures and fish (social + spawning):** Creatures maintain their own population floor (50) and cap (120) within per-species constraints, mate within 64 units, clash within 58 units, and flee from conflict. They're born near verdant areas (plants) and consume buggy creatures that land in ponds (bugs become fish food). Their movement every tick is driven by goal-seeking (feed/drink/rest/roam drives cycling every 5 ticks, seeded-desynced so they don't all do the same thing at once).
- **Giant (the gardener NPC):** Walks the world every tick, tending an ordered sequence of jobs graded by need÷distance: ripening young plants, thinning over-crowded patches, filling marks, fusing pebbles, breaking over-sized boulders, and sowing seeds where it's sparse. Its writes (mutations to objects) happen outside the tick ledger, immediately, so its work isn't subject to the checkpoint decouple.
- **Population ceiling (10,000 objects, tunable):** When the world is full, isolation fade and ceiling trim work together to make room: forgotten objects fade naturally, and the longest-untouched are evicted if the world is saturated. The ceiling has breathing room (trimmed to 2 under the cap, CEIL_TRIM) so a packed world stays churning rather than freezing.
- **Water flow and pond geometry (POOLS, POND_ASPECT, elliptical):** Free objects near a pool drift along the flow field (seeded, shared with the client render), which bends around stones (channelling) and toward cooler areas (heat-gradient deflection). Plants and stones that end up in water are nudged to the bank each tick. Fish are constrained to swim within ponds, and creatures that land in water become fish food.
- **Anomalies (rare luminous wonders):** Spawn rarely (3% per tick per mature plant, only in Growing/Rising seasons, capped at 4 at once). They accelerate growth and slow aging within 200 units (ANOMALY_RADIUS), and can ripen/burst plants or tame/glow creatures when dropped nearby. They fuse when placed on each other, creating hybrids with combined powers that never wear out or fade — they're persistent until deliberately dissolved.
- **Write-economy ledger (TickContext, dirty set, checkpoint):** Every change flows through one TickContext per tick, separating broadcast-only changes (deferred to checkpoint), reclaimed holds (immediate), spawns (immediate), and deaths (immediate). The dirty set tracks objects whose in-memory state diverged from disk since their last persist. The checkpoint (every 30 minutes wall-clock) flushes only dirty objects, so write cost scales with change, not population.

> **Where the thread is thin →** The giant's immediate writes (outside the tick ledger) are a one-way street: it reads the world state but its work never feeds back into the tick's RNG or write-accounting directly (it writes through DO forwarders, not the TickContext). This asymmetry means the giant's efficiency could degrade if it operated at a different cadence or had higher latency — a tighter feedback loop (e.g. the giant's work incrementing a "tended" counter each tick) would let the population system adapt faster to how much the gardener is actually keeping things in balance. The spatial grid (GRID_CELL = 256 world units) is purely in-memory and never persisted, so it's rebuilt from scratch on every DO restart (line 1483). This is fine (rebuilding is O(n log n) and cheap), but there's no optimization for a world that's been idle — the grid still gets rebuilt even if nothing has moved. A persisted grid would survive restart with zero cost, but it would add complexity. The density-dependent shedding (SHED_DENSITY_R/SOFT/MAX) suppresses crowding outward, but it only looks at same-family plants within a fixed radius. Different plant families don't count as "crowding" to each other (only to themselves), so if someone manually plants two very different families on top of each other, they'll both shed freely. The algorithm treats family diversity as an absence of crowding, even if the total object density is very high. Presences (people in the world) are tracked by their ephemeral pid (per-connection), not by identity or token, so if the same person reconnects, they're a fresh presence with a fresh warmth history. This is intentional (no identity tracking), but it means a disconnected person's lingering warmth benefit stops immediately — their old presence remains "stale" (PRESENCE_STALE_MS = 12 seconds) and then is pruned (line 1383-1386). A user who waits just over 12 seconds between disconnecting and reconnecting loses their warmth credit without notice. The communion system (people tending a patch together → the world blossoms) is purely in-memory and event-driven: it tracks presence pairs within COMMUNION_R (300 units) that linger together for 2+ ticks, spawns flowers and maybe an anomaly, then waits 4 ticks before it can bloom again in the same spot. But there's no visualization of the communion trigger itself — watchers see the bloom appear but not the heartbeat of togetherness that caused it. The system is invisible (no "communion meter" or signal), so its power to create shared moments is felt but never explicitly named.

## Part 2 — the web as it stands today

Seven kinds of thing (seeds, stones, creatures, fish, crystals, anomalies, marks) share the world, held together by a handful of forces. The **strong loops** already turning today:

- **Warmth → growth.** Heat from people (and from the thermal field) speeds a plant's maturing — ~18 minutes with someone lingering vs. ~10 hours alone. Your attention is literally what makes things grow.
- **Crowding → spread.** A plant sheds freely with few neighbours, not at all when hemmed in (9+ within 160 units) — so groves push *outward* instead of thickening into thickets.
- **Seasons → rhythm.** The four-season clock speeds and slows growth and aging for the whole world at once.
- **The gardeners → balance.** Two tireless giants walk the world scoring imbalances by need÷distance — ripening, thinning, sowing, fusing pebbles, breaking boulders.
- **Anomalies → local wonder.** A rare luminous thing makes a ~200-unit halo where growth quickens and aging slows.
- **Communion → blossom.** Two people lingering together for a couple of ticks make the world bloom between them.
- **Forgetting → turnover.** Untended stones crumble; a full world trims its oldest-untouched things — so the world keeps breathing.

The trouble — and the opportunity — is that most of these loops run *one way*, and several families barely touch each other at all.

### The thin seams — where things live *in parallel* rather than *affecting each other*

- Water Flow ↔ Drift: one-directional. Plants creep along current but drift never feeds back.
- Fish ↔ Creatures: unidirectional consumption. Crawlers become food but fish never feed creatures.
- Stones ↔ Creatures: no mutual influence. Creatures rest beside stones but stones never respond.
- Stones ↔ Anomalies: completely decoupled. Anomalies ignore stones; no cross-family interaction.
- Heat Field ↔ Creatures: one-way. Heat accelerates plants but creatures never sense warmth.
- Creatures ↔ Plants: feeding only. Creatures eat plants but plants never stress-respond to browsing.
- Giant ↔ Creatures: pure observation. Gardener watches but never herds or influences creatures.
- Anomalies ↔ Discovery: random without reciprocity. Anomalies spawn by chance; world doesn't reward seeking.
- Seasons ↔ Creature Behavior: decoupled. Creatures' drives don't respond to seasonal pressure.
- Isolation Fade ↔ Ecosystem: stone-only. Only stones crumble; ecosystem isn't self-cleaning at scale.
- Creature Breeding ↔ Environment: isolated. Breeding rates don't respond to resource scarcity or anomalies.
- Water Flow ↔ Stones: one-way. Flow bends around stones but stones never erode from flowing water.

## Part 3 — ideas to thicken the web into a living ecosystem

Ranked by impact × fit-with-Drift (calm, wordless, slow, every-object-unique, server-authoritative, and cheap on writes — the Durable Object must never write per-object-per-tick). Each turns a one-way link into a **loop**, or gives a visitor's actions **ecological consequences**.

### 1. Creatures Sense & Seek Warmth  
*Effort: Medium*

- **The loop it creates:** Heat (presence) ↔ Creature Attraction ↔ Congregation ↔ Visible Warmth Halos
- **Who it links:** Heat Field → Creatures. Creatures read heat grid and drift toward warm zones during roam. Warmth decay (~1h) means creatures follow thermal trails.
- **What a visitor feels:** Visitors see creatures gather as they stand still. Warmth becomes a magnet. Tended patches visibly congregate creatures, marking care received.
- **Watch-out:** Zero new writes; creatures read existing heat grid. Movement defers via checkpoint. No write-economy risk.

### 2. Plants Attract Creatures via Browsing Pressure  
*Effort: Medium*

- **The loop it creates:** Plants (seeding) ↔ Creature Grazing ↔ Browsed Counter ↔ Shed Rate Modulation ↔ Foraging Pressure
- **Who it links:** Creatures → Plants. Creatures lingering >3 ticks increment `browsed` counter. Heavily browsed plants shed at 50% rate.
- **What a visitor feels:** Creatures visibly graze patches. Grove with many creatures shows visible thinning. Planting seeds becomes challenge against browser pressure.
- **Watch-out:** `browsed` counter on seeds (minor storage); increments deferred via checkpoint. Low risk if tuned carefully.

### 3. Seasonal Creature Behavior Shift  
*Effort: Small*

- **The loop it creates:** Season ↔ Creature Drive Timing ↔ Survival Pressure ↔ Emergent Scarcity/Abundance
- **Who it links:** Season → Creatures. Shorten feed-cycle in Resting (2 ticks, desperate) while growth freezes; lengthen in Growing (4 ticks, abundant).
- **What a visitor feels:** In Resting, creatures hunt scarce plants desperately. In Growing, abundance and calm. Each season tells different ecological story.
- **Watch-out:** Zero writes; just read season phase and modulate drive cycle timing. No risk.

### 4. Anomalies Attract Creatures & Relax Shedding  
*Effort: Medium*

- **The loop it creates:** Anomaly ↔ Creature Seeking ↔ Clustering ↔ Plant Shedding Relax ↔ Sacred Grove Formation
- **Who it links:** Anomaly → Creatures & Plants. Creatures detect anomalies within 500 units and drift toward them. Plants near anomalies have relaxed density suppression.
- **What a visitor feels:** Anomalies become visible hubs of life. Creatures gather, plants cluster. Shifts from invisible mechanic to felt landmark and destination.
- **Watch-out:** Zero writes; creatures read anomaly list (gathered each tick), plants read proximity (already checked). No risk.

### 5. Stones Settle Naturally & Cluster  
*Effort: Medium*

- **The loop it creates:** Stones ↔ Gravity/Settling ↔ Weak Attraction ↔ Emergent Clusters & Landmarks
- **Who it links:** Stone Motion → Space. Stones drift downward (~2 units/tick) and weakly attract nearby stones (~0.1 units/tick within 200 units).
- **What a visitor feels:** Scattered stones form natural clusters and rock fields over time. Long-term players watch geomorphology emerge organically.
- **Watch-out:** Drift uses existing infrastructure; only broadcast on POS_BCAST_DELTA=6 units. Checkpoint flushes. No write-economy risk.

### 6. Stones Erode from Water Flow  
*Effort: Large*

- **The loop it creates:** Water Flow ↔ Stone Erosion ↔ Pebble Formation ↔ Natural Wear Cycle
- **Who it links:** Water → Stones. Stones drifting in flow lose 1 handling durability per 30 ticks, representing water-wear.
- **What a visitor feels:** Stones aren't immortal; flow gently wears them. Player watches stone in water slowly shrink. Landscape turns over naturally.
- **Watch-out:** Handling counter already stored and deferred. Erosion increments deferred via checkpoint. Low risk.

### 7. Water Flow Responds to Population Density  
*Effort: Large*

- **The loop it creates:** Density ↔ Flow Speed ↔ Drift Acceleration ↔ Population Spreading
- **Who it links:** Population → Flow → Drift. Flow speed increases when pool >100 objects (1.5×) or >200 objects (2.0×); decreases to 0.8× when <10.
- **What a visitor feels:** Player notices flow shift as pool fills. Crowded pool churns fast and auto-disperses objects. World self-organizes via flow pressure.
- **Watch-out:** Zero writes; flow speed computed at drift-time from current object count. No risk.

### 8. Creatures Breed Near Anomalies → Marked Lineages  
*Effort: Medium*

- **The loop it creates:** Anomaly ↔ Breeding Ground ↔ Marked Offspring ↔ Heritable Variation ↔ Visible Lineages
- **Who it links:** Anomaly → Creatures → Breeding. Offspring mating within 200 units of anomaly: 20% chance marked shimmer trait. Marked creatures breed 6× faster.
- **What a visitor feels:** Anomalies become evolutionary centers. Shimmer-family clusters around anomalies. World develops visible bloodlines and heritable memory.
- **Watch-out:** Offspring records already spawn (immediate write); `marked` boolean is simple field. Very low risk.

### 9. Browsed Plants Burst Under Pressure  
*Effort: Medium*

- **The loop it creates:** Creature Browsing ↔ Plant Stress ↔ Seed Burst → Mutualism
- **Who it links:** Creatures → Plants → Burst. Plant with browsed >8 enters stress; every 6 ticks, 25% chance to burst into 2–4 saplings then dissolve.
- **What a visitor feels:** Heavy grazing triggers explosive reproduction. Browsed plants 'give up' and spread. Creature-plant becomes mutualistic. Creature-rich zone good for seed diversity.
- **Watch-out:** Burst is removal+spawn (already writes); `stressed` boolean minimal. Medium risk if burst too frequent; tuning critical.

### 10. Giant's Tending Becomes Visible  
*Effort: Medium*

- **The loop it creates:** Giant Activity ↔ Visible Shimmer Auras ↔ Felt Agency ↔ Player-NPC Collaboration
- **Who it links:** Giant → Visible Trail. When giant ripens/thins/breaks, emit ephemeral shimmer aura (~30s). Maintains visible 'just-tended' list.
- **What a visitor feels:** Giant's logic becomes felt agency, not hidden mechanics. Player reads landscape as record of giant's priorities. Implicit collaborators.
- **Watch-out:** Auras ephemeral and in-memory (no persistence). Just-tended list in-memory only. Zero new persistent writes. No risk.

### 11. Communion Creates Persistent Patch Memory  
*Effort: Medium*

- **The loop it creates:** Communion Bloom ↔ Persistent Patch Memory ↔ Long-Term Echo ↔ Returning Pair Bonus
- **Who it links:** Communion → Persistent Reward. On bloom, record patch memory. Active memories (<0.5 decay) +0.01 maturity/tick. Same pair within fresh memory: faster re-bloom.
- **What a visitor feels:** Patches retain echo of past blooms. If same two return, world remembers and blooms again faster. Felt story: where we first bloomed together.
- **Watch-out:** High if many unique patches accumulate; storage risk. Mitigate with cap (100 max). Medium-to-high risk; monitor aggressively.

### 12. Anomalies Drift Toward Creature Clusters  
*Effort: Medium*

- **The loop it creates:** Creature Clustering ↔ Anomaly Drift ↔ Sacred Grove Migration ↔ Emergent Hotspots
- **Who it links:** Creatures ↔ Anomalies. Free anomalies drift (~3 units/tick) toward clusters (>5 creatures within 400 units). If idle, drift back to world COG.
- **What a visitor feels:** Anomalies slowly colonize thriving zones. Visitor tending creatures over time finds anomaly drifting there. Long-term: sacred groves form naturally.
- **Watch-out:** Drift uses existing infrastructure; only broadcast on POS_BCAST_DELTA. Checkpoint flushes. No new writes. No risk.
